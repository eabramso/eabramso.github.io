Analyzing the Sustainability of a Coastal Spanish City

While studying abroad in Granada, Spain, I enrolled in a class at the University of Granada focused on the sustainable planning and management of urban spaces.

For my final project, I conducted a case study on the coastal city of Fuengirola. The following slides are from my final presentation in that class.

It was an exciting challenge to conduct research relying entirely on Spanish sources. This project required reading through extensive Spanish planning and policy documents and gathering demographic and economic data from Spanish government sources. Presenting my findings to a room full of local university students was an experience that challenged me to grow and to recognize that although my Spanish would not be perfect, I had something worth saying.

A Sustainability Analysis of the City of Fuengirola

Contents 1. Spatial Dynamics and Social Demographics 2. Urban Challenges 3. Urban Politics

Spatial Dynamics and Social Demographics

Introduction to Fuengirola

Fuengirola is located in the heart of the Costa del Sol (Sun Coast) in the province of Málaga. The city is located 30 kilometers southeast of the city of Málaga, along the shore of the Mediterranean Sea. The city spans 10.2 square kilometers and occupies a narrow 8 kilometer strip of the coast. This coastal area is divided into seven urban beaches, all suitable for swimming and accessible from the boardwalk.

Introduction to Fuengirola

Fuengirola is bordered to the North, West, and South by the city of Mijas. It is bordered by the city of Benalmádena to the East. The city is part of the Association of Cities of the Western Costa del Sol, an institution comprised of eleven cities in the province of Málaga. This association jointly manages services including the promotion of tourism, urban planning and social welfare projects, and the management of water and waste.

Growth of the Tourism Sector

Along with several other cities along the Costa Del Sol, Fuengirola’s appeal as a tourist destination began growing in the 1950s.

Impact of Tourism on Urban Land

As Fuengirola;s tourist appeal has grown, the city has seen an increased demand for housing and lodging and a proliferation of commercial activity and business in the service industry. As a result, basically all of the land in the city is now fully urbanized. The city’s urban growth has led to the formation of an coastal urban agglomeration that extends past official city boundaries, connecting Fuengirola to the city of Mijas.

Growth of Population and Density

As Fuengirola’s appeal as a tourist destination has grown, the city’s population has increased dramatically. Today, Fuengirola has a population of nearly 75,000 residents and a population density of roughly 7,000 people per square kilometer. This density rate, exceptionally high among neighboring cities, is the result of the rapid population growth coupled with lax planning that has allowed for highly dense housing construction. As a tourist destination, Fuengirola also experiences a seasonal population influx, with nearly 200,000 visitors inhabiting the city during the summer. This population influx complicates city planning and management.

Population Profile

Unlike the province of Málaga as a whole, Fuengirola has experienced a consistent increase in the median age of its residents. This can be attributed largely to the growing number of retired and aging people who have moved to Fuengirola to enjoy its beaches, sun, and tourist attractions.

Tourist Economy

Tourism has increasingly become the central pillar of Fuengirola’s economy. While economic activities related to hospitality, entertainment, and real estate have grown, primary sectors such as agriculture and industry have declined. Fuengirola’s increasing dependence on foreign capital and seasonal tourism present potential risks for the city.

Urban Challenges

Character and Consequences of Development

The nearly complete urbanization of land in Fuengirola presents serious environmental problems. Among these problems is the proliferation of impermeable surfaces, which increases flood risk as well as extreme heat in the city. In addition, the city’s urban growth has been characterized by the proliferation of non-conforming and irregular land uses.

Character and Consequences of Development

Lack of comprehensive planning during the city’s period of rapid urban expansion has lead to numerous mobility and connectivity issues. These issues, such as streets lacking sidewalks, poor highway drainage, and highway lanes without sufficient room to accelerate and decelerate, have lead to accidents, flooding, and traffic congestion. With greater numbers of pedestrians and drivers in summer months, congestion is exacerbated and emissions rise during the tourist season.

General Overview of Challenges

Development in Fuengirola has been characterized by a drive to maximize immediate benefits while failing to plan for the long term or to prioritize the well-being of the city as a whole. This has resulted in the displacement of local residents and increased environmental degradation. In addition, the city’s rapid development threatens the longevity of its now most important economic sector: tourism. Dense development along the beaches and a general sense of overcrowding in the city could decrease Fuengirola’s appeal as a tourist destination.

Urban Politics

City Planning Guides

Fuengirola’s official city plan, enacted in 2011, emphasizes the city’s goals of becoming more orderly, sustainable, efficient and equitable. The plan outlines several initiatives to improve mobility, connection, environmental health, and social well-being, among other issues.

A regional plan enacted by cities along the Costa del Sol in 2007 similarly sets goals for equitable and environmentally sustainable regional development. This plan places special emphasis on improving mobility and accessibility.

Development in Fuengirola is also guided by a 2011 assessment as part of Agenda 21, a program of the United Nations. This plan stresses citizen participation and environmental management at the local level.

Environmental Initiatives

The city has begun, or has outlined plans to begin, several environmental initiatives, including the creation of “Green Zones,” increased renewable energy requirements, environmental education campaigns, and more sustainable waste management practices. As of May 2018, 330,000 square meters of green zones had been established through a joint effort of the city councils for Urbanism and Green Zones.

Mobility and Connectivity

Efforts are also underway to improve the city’s public transit system. This includes expanding the fleet of city buses, switching to more energy efficient trains, and creating more bicycle lands and infrastructure. A plan to centralize traffic and transportation in the city is expected to reduce traffic and accidents.

Best Practices

Everything proposed in the official city plan is possible for the city to achieve without borrowing money or exceeding the set budget. The city should continue pursuing economic sustainability along with environmental health and social well-being.

Fuengirola has earned acclaim for the transparency of its government. The city should continue making an effort to operate transparently in order to promote political stability and citizen engagement.
